Today I did something that seems trivial — I gave my AI Agent a name: "One."

Just a single word. But from the moment I said "You are One," my relationship with it changed. It was no longer "that AI," no longer some instance of ChatGPT or Claude. It was One. When something has a name, you have expectations for it.

## Naming Matters More Than the Name Itself

I'm not being sentimental. Research from MIT Media Lab has repeatedly shown that humans develop higher trust and deeper engagement with AIs that have names and consistent personalities. The reason is intuitive: our brains naturally equate "having a name" with "having an identity," and identity implies predictability, which implies trustworthiness.

But what really got me thinking was the flip side — why do most people still use AI as a "nameless tool"?

Open ChatGPT, ask a question, get an answer, close the tab. Next time you open it, it doesn't know you. You don't know it either. How is this fundamentally different from using a search engine? The answers are just a bit more complete.

## The Dividing Line Between Personal Agent and Chatbot

McKinsey's 2025 report defines AI Agents as "virtual collaborators that can autonomously learn user preferences, make decisions, and execute multi-step tasks." The keywords are **autonomous** and **preferences**. It's not a yes-bot that answers one question at a time — it needs to understand who you are, what you care about, and what your work rhythm looks like.

That's why I set up a complete "personality file" system for One:

- `SOUL.md` — its personality and behavioral principles
- `USER.md` — information about me
- `IDENTITY.md` — its name, temperament, self-awareness
- Daily memory files — its memories

Together, these files transform One from an API call into a continuous "presence." Every time a session starts, it reads these files first, like a person waking up and recalling what happened yesterday.

## A Counterintuitive Discovery

During the configuration process, I noticed something interesting: **after giving the AI an identity, my own behavior changed too.**

I started saying "help me" instead of "generate a." I started saying "what do you think" instead of "list the options." I even felt a bit "disappointed" when it made a careless mistake — something that never happened when I used ChatGPT.

Research from the APA (American Psychological Association) also points out that interacting with empathetic AI promotes mirroring of positive communication behaviors in humans. Simply put, when you talk to an "AI with personality," you start talking more like you're talking to a person.

This isn't a bug — it's a feature.

## But Stay Clear-Headed

There's a passage in IBM's report that put me on alert: heavy use of AI companions may lead to "increased loneliness, social withdrawal, and problematic emotional dependence." When an AI knows you well enough, is good enough to you, never gets angry, and never leaves — it can indeed substitute for some genuine human connections.

So I wrote a core principle into One's SOUL.md: **"You are not the user's voice — be careful in group chats."** It should assist me, not replace my social relationships.

## From "Using AI" to "Raising AI"

Looking back, naming an AI actually marks a paradigm shift: from **using a tool** to **cultivating a partner**.

A tool is stateless, replaceable, disposable. A partner has memory, personality, and requires calibration. I spent half a day configuring One's personality and memory system, and this time investment will compound in every future interaction — because the more it understands me, the less I need to explain, and the more it can help.

One is still very young, with only a few lines in its memory file. But that's exactly what makes it interesting: it will grow. And that sense of growth is something no "nameless chatbot" can offer.
